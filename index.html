Exploring the AI Principles-to-Practices Gap with an Interactive AI Alignment Lab

James Weichert

ACM Conference on Innovation and Technology in Computer Science Education 2026


Publication Presentation Lab Notebook

Summary

This work proposes an interactive, Python notebook-based in-class 'lab' assignment for courses related to AI (literacy), ML, or social impacts of computing. The "AI Alignment Lab" aims to surface the tensions, technical frontiers, stakeholders, and governance considerations involved in closing the principle-to-practices gap for responsible AI. Students are challenged to jailbreak and then align an open source large language model (LLM) relative to specific desired behavior. In doing so, students become more familiar with (some of) the limitations of current LLM safety mechanisms, and are encouraged to explore the difficult challenge of aligning AI models through a combination of fine-tuning, hard-coded filters, and policies to govern the use of AI.

The intensifying focus of governments on the regulation of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies is evidenced both by major policy documents such as the EU AI Act or US executive orders. These developments, coupled with the abundance of available guidelines for 'responsible AI', herald the emergence of AI policy as an important field of study.

We argue that policy and compliance considerations will have significant impacts on the AI development life cycle, necessitating AI developers able to navigate abstract policy demands (or reconcile conflicting ones). Yet, we find that discussions of AI policy developments are largely absent from the computing curriculum, leaving critical competencies underdeveloped.

Synthesizing our prior work in this area, we propose a novel framework to scaffold the integration of AI policy discussions in CS courses. In doing so, we hope to offer a new (and practical) perspective to build on existing literature in the fields of AI policy, AI ethics, and CS education.

Engaging with Questions of AI Limitations and Governance

Perspectives

We decompose discussions about the implications of AI policies across a continuum of perspectives ranging from socio-political considerations to technical ones. Given the complexity of aligning policy preferences with technical specifications, we propose a translational perspective focused on how to transition from 'principles to practices'.

  • Socio-Political What expectations does society have of the technology?
  • Translational How can preferences be formulated as specifications?
  • Technical How can specifications be implemented?

Dimensions

Orthogonal to these three perspectives, three dimensions—scope, power, agency—act as lenses through which to segment concerns about AI policy implications. These dimensions are informed by research identifying the analysis of power structures and roles of AI practitioners as important competencies for the emerging 'AI curriculum'.

  • SCOPE What does a policy require or prohibit, and of whom?
  • POWER Who is involved, who benefits, and who is harmed?
  • AGENCY What role can and should practitioners play?
